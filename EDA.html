Data analysis¶

1. Exploratory Data Analysis¶

Before doing any classification, we can look at what kind of information the scraped data gives us in itself. In our exploratory data analysis, we want to detect any obvious differences in how different universities handle gender and minorities. At first sight, how important is it to apply early or to have a family member alumnus?

When creating and evaluating graphs based on our scraped data, we should keep in mind that our dataset is not balanced. We simply have a lot more data for some universities than for others. This is shown below.

Gender¶

Firstly, we immediately notice the presence of 4 California universities in the top 5 when it comes to accepting females. UCLA and UCB are all known as more technical universities, so it could be the case that these universities are overcompensating for a historically poor gender split by admitting more females to level out the ratio. On the other side, Stanford, Vanderbilt and Duke seem to be doing quite poorly on the gender equality front, with only about 40% of admitted students being female in our dataset.

Minority¶

The percentage of accepted minorities is very similar in most universities, hovering around 25%. Whereas the Californian universities were better than the East Coast universities in terms of female admittance, they are worse for minority admittance. We find it troublesome that the UCs, public universities that pride themselves on diversity of accepted candidates, are relatively poor in minority acceptance. Caltech is clearly an outlier here, likely due to its lack of data. For Notre Dame on the other hand, we do have enough records of acceptances so we can conclude that within the group of admitted students, there is a low percentage of minorities compared to that of other schools. Given that Notre Dame also appears to be slightly gender-biased (from the Gender plot), it seems that the university is not actively anti-discriminating their applicants.

Alumni¶

How important is legacy status? Our graph shows that there are indeed universities for which it matters more than for others. For most universities, only about 5% of accepted students have an alumni connection. However, for the universities that are generally considered more elitist (e.g. Stanford and the Ivy Leagues), having legacy status does appear to improve one's chances.

Early application¶

Early application seems to play an enormous role at some colleges, specifically MIT (~60%) and Chicago, Yale, Georgetown, and Notre Dame (~40%). In contrast, the public Californian universities give almost no weight to whether an application was early. For the UCs, < 1% of admittied students sent in early applications.

Out of state¶

Our graph shows that for many universities, almost all accepted students are from a different state than the university. The Californian universities have the lowest proportion of out-of-state students, but this can be understood as an artifact of California being the most populous state and therefore having the most applicants. This may explain why, despite the fact that Caltech and Stanford are private, they still admit many Californians. As is expected, the UCs have the lowest proportion of out-of-state students, as the institutions are public and known to give preference to California residents. On the other side of the spectrum, Georgetown achieves a percentage of 99.41 out of state students. Again, this is to be expected given it is located in the state of "District of Columbia".

International¶

It is notable that the universities that admit the most (in terms of proportion) international students are those that are widely considered to be the best: Stanford, MIT, and the Ivy Leagues (especially Columbia and Harvard). This probably has two main reasons. Firstly, these are the universities with the best international reputation, so they almost certainly receive more applications from international students compared to other universities. Secondly, these universities are all private, so are less likely to favor in-state and American students. They will just pick the best candidates from across the world.

Sports¶

High-school varsity sport plays an important role in the application to several top schools-- notably Harvard, MIT, Stanford. In general (and this is by no means a hard-and-fast rule), the universities with a better "reputation" take sport into account more. The notable exception to this rule is the group of UCs, where < 2% admitted applicants listed proficiency at sport. Interestingly, none of Northwestern's admitted students listed varsity sport, and this is not for lack of data from applications to this college.

Public/Private school¶

Overall, universities' admitted students are 10-30% private-school high-school students, with the notable exception of Notre Dame (~40%). Recall that Notre Dame was also poor for gender equality and minority acceptance, and this plot shows it accepts more (in proportion) private-school students than the other colleges. Additionally, however, Notre Dame is a religious school, and may thus favor applicants from parochial high schools. Not surprisingly, the UCs have the lowest proportion of private-school students, given that they are public universities.

Gender & Racial Equality¶

Now, let us concentrate on the two most discussed indicators in college admissions: gender and minority race. Instead of just looking at the percentages admitted by universities, we should check if there are any discrepencies in how their scores compare. Ideally, we want the scores of minorities and non-minorities (or males and females) to be completely balanced. However, it is generally thought that the SAT is racially biased, so universities should be compensating for this with more lenience towards minorities.

Private vs. Public school¶

An important question for parents all over America is: Should I send my child to private school? Is it worth spending so much money on tuition? On the one hand, we could check if having been to a private high school gives one an edge in applications. That is, for similar candidates when it comes to test scores, gender, or race, is the private school candidate favored above the public school candidate? This analysis however goes beyond exploratory data analysis. Above, we investigated the percentage of accepted private school candidates for each university, but this result does not reflect the importance universities attribute to the high school type, as it is correlated to several other variables, such as GPA and admission test scores. What we can do at this point is investigate these correlations. Does sending your child to private school result in higher GPA or test scores?

The answer to this question is clearly yes. The graphs above show that private school students will obtain an overall higher admissions test score, a higher GPA, and most notably, higher AP exam scores than public school students. One could remark that the higher GPA is a result of private schools grading students more leniently, but admissions tests and AP exams are standardised, so internal grading should not be the cause of the disparity.

It is worth mentioning that there is a confounding factor here. In general, students who go to private schools are wealthier. This means their families can afford private tuition for exams and admission tests, which clearly can boost results. Moreover, these students are less likely to require financial aid for college tuition, which may make the university more likely to accept them.

There are several useful insights from the correlation heatmap above, some more noticeable and important than others.

  • Firstly, we see that the upper left corner, consisting of academic test features, is positively correlated. Intuitively this makes sense, as it indicates that in general, students with a higher GPA do better at admissions tests and APs.

  • The second interesting feature is that the bottom left corner is negatively correlated. This indicates that students with higher academic results tend to apply to smaller private schools with smaller acceptance rate. Again this makes sense, as the students with better results would apply to more elite schools.

  • Thirdly, and not unsurprisingly, size and public are negatively correlated with out of state and positively correlated with acceptrate. So the larger public schools have a larger acceptance rate (almost by definition), and take less out of state students. For example, the UC system accept more students, but favour Californian students. Furthermore, the standout color of the plot is the high positive correlation between size and public. As expected, public schools are more likely to be larger.

Remarks On Sampling Bias¶

It was stated earlier that for some colleges, we have insufficient data to make conclusive insights. Specifically, the data we scraped does not have sufficient records of students accepted to Caltech, so any insight regarding Caltech is misleading.

Another, potentially more concerning problem, is that of sampling bias. There is no evidence to suggest that the posts on College Data are truthful. There may be a positive skew to the data because students are more likely to exaggerate than downplay their scores. This means scores may be incorrect, and more importantly, have a bias towards higher values. There is, unfortunately, not much we can do to combat this bias, since it is nigh on impossible to estimate the magnitude of the bias.

Let's assume (probably incorrectly) that all posts on collegedata.com are truthful. There is nevertheless a selection bias. The students who post on College Data are unlikely to be a fair cross-section of students applying to college. These students are pro-actively comparing colleges, doing research, seeking feedback, and in general are more likely to be on the high-achieving end of students.

To see this, below is a histogram of the GPAs reported on College Data, unnormalized. We see that almost 7,000 of the 16,000 results are a 4.0 GPA. Without further exploration, we know there is a sampling bias, since the proportion of perfect GPAs should be much lower in a population data set.

The problem in our sampling means that the training data for our algorithm is biased. Again, as this is the only dataset we really had time to consider (and is one of the few free readily-available online sources), there is not much we can do to account for this. It is a weakness of the model. However, it is not as significant a weakness as might be expected.

Firstly, the universities we are considering are the top 25 universities in the US, so will in general only accept the high-achieving students with excellent GPAs. So in some ways the bias in the universities we consider accounts for the bias in the students we consider. (This is not a rigorous defence, but an intuitive explanation as to why the selection bias may not be too much of a hindrance.) Secondly, the students using our website will most likely be of a similar ilk to those who post on collegedata.com, namely high-achieving students who are proactively looking for ways to improve and quantify their acceptance chances.

In conclusion, our model is plagued with selection bias. However, there is not a whole lot we can do so account for the bias, and the bias should not affect the model significantly enough as to render it meaningless.
